# The Science of Human Intelligence: A Book-Length Research Compilation

## TL;DR
- **Intelligence is real, measurable, and consequential**: a general factor (g) emerges robustly from the positive correlations among cognitive tests, is organized hierarchically (Cattell-Horn-Carroll), is highly heritable in adulthood (~0.7–0.8; the Wilson effect), and predicts education, job performance, health, and longevity — though the magnitude of its predictive validity for job performance is now actively contested (Schmidt & Hunter's r ≈ .51 vs. Sackett et al. 2022's downward revision to ~.31 and Griebe et al. 2022's ~.22).
- **The biology is distributed and the effects are small**: intelligence maps onto a parieto-frontal network (P-FIT) and whole-brain network efficiency, but single brain–behavior correlations are modest (brain volume r ≈ .24) and the field is correcting for a reproducibility crisis (Marek et al. 2022 showed reliable brain-wide associations require thousands of subjects).
- **Intelligence is both heritable and malleable**: genetics explains roughly half of variance (polygenic, thousands of variants; best polygenic scores explain ~7–16%), yet education reliably raises IQ (1–5 points/year; Ritchie & Tucker-Drob 2018), the Flynn effect shows large environmental gains of ~2.31 standard-score points per decade (and recent reversals), while commercial "brain training" shows no convincing far transfer (Melby-Lervåg, Redick & Hulme 2016).

## Key Findings
This compilation organizes the science into seven book-chapter-ready domains. The strongest-supported claims: the existence and stability of g; the increasing heritability of intelligence with age; the predictive validity of cognitive ability for important life outcomes (even at revised lower estimates); the failure of brain training to produce far transfer; and the causal effect of education on measured intelligence. The most contested or weakly-supported: the precise magnitude of cognitive ability's validity for job performance; the size and meaning of specific brain–behavior correlations; gene-by-SES interaction (Scarr-Rowe) replication; and any claims about group differences, where mainstream consensus emphasizes environmental explanations and genuine uncertainty.

## Details

### CHAPTER 1 — Concept and Structure of Intelligence

**Spearman's g and the positive manifold.** Charles Spearman (1904) observed that all cognitive tests correlate positively (the "positive manifold") and extracted a general factor, g. This remains the most replicated finding in differential psychology.

**The hierarchical synthesis (CHC).** The dominant modern framework is Cattell-Horn-Carroll theory. It integrates Raymond Cattell's (1941) distinction between fluid intelligence (Gf, reasoning with novel material) and crystallized intelligence (Gc, accumulated knowledge), which John Horn expanded into 8–10 broad abilities, with John Carroll's three-stratum theory. Carroll's *Human Cognitive Abilities* (1993) reanalyzed more than 460 datasets spanning the 20th century, yielding a three-layer hierarchy: Stratum III (g), Stratum II (~10 broad abilities including Gf, Gc, visual processing Gv, working memory Gsm/Gwm, processing speed Gs), and Stratum I (70+ narrow abilities). Kevin McGrew formalized the CHC synthesis in the late 1990s; it structures the WJ-IV, WISC-V, KABC-II, and SB5. A recent reanalysis (PMC9959556, 2023) argues the framework should be reframed as a "family of obliquely correlated CHC theories," and that bifactor analyses support Carroll's three-stratum model over alternatives.

**Working memory, processing speed, executive function.** Working memory capacity correlates strongly with Gf (often r ≈ .7–.8 at the latent level in some studies), prompting debate over whether they are identical. Process-overlap theory (Kovacs & Conway) and mutualism/network models (van der Maas et al. 2006) propose that g is not a single causal entity but emerges from overlapping processes or mutually reinforcing developmental interactions among initially independent abilities — directly relevant to the Barbey Network Neuroscience Theory below.

**Competing frameworks.** Gardner's multiple intelligences and Sternberg's triarchic theory remain popular but are widely critiqued for lacking psychometric support. Emotional intelligence splits into ability models (Mayer-Salovey) with modest validity and "mixed" models criticized as repackaged personality. The g-VPR model (Johnson & Bouchard) adds verbal-perceptual-rotation strata beneath g. Spearman's "law of diminishing returns" (ability differentiation) — that g is less dominant at higher ability — has mixed empirical support.

### CHAPTER 2 — Individual Differences and Predictive Validity

**The job-performance controversy (a genuinely contested area).** Schmidt & Hunter (1998, *Psychological Bulletin*; ~85 years of selection research) reported general mental ability (GMA) as the best single predictor of job performance, with operational validity r ≈ .51 overall, rising to r ≈ .58 for high-complexity jobs and falling to r ≈ .23 for low-complexity jobs. This was the field's consensus for two decades. **It is now seriously challenged.** Sackett, Zhang, Berry & Lievens (2022, *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 107(11), 2040–2068) argued that prior meta-analyses systematically overcorrected for range restriction (especially applying predictive-study corrections to concurrent studies), revising GMA's corrected validity downward to ~.31. Griebe et al. (2022) meta-analyzed 113 21st-century validity studies (153 samples, N = 40,740) and found a mean observed validity of just .16, rising to .22 corrected — dropping cognitive ability's rank among predictors substantially. Defenders (Ones & Viswesvaran 2023; Oh et al. 2023) counter that concurrent and predictive designs have similar range restriction. **The honest summary: cognitive ability remains a meaningful predictor, especially for complex jobs and training, but its preeminence and exact magnitude are now actively disputed.**

**Education, income, and the gifted.** Cognitive ability predicts educational attainment and income robustly. The Study of Mathematically Precocious Youth (SMPY; Lubinski & Benbow, founded 1971 by Julian Stanley) tracked >5,000 participants for 50+ years. Kell, Lubinski & Benbow (2013, *Psychological Science*, 24(5), 648–659) showed that even within the top 1%, ability differences at age 13 predicted differential adult accomplishment. Lubinski, Benbow & Kell (2014, *Psychological Science*, 25(12), 2217–2232) followed 1,650 top-1% participants four decades later: 4.1% earned tenure at major research universities, 2.3% became top executives at Fortune 500/"name brand" companies, and the cohort had published 85 books and 7,572 refereed articles and secured 681 patents — far exceeding base rates. Makel et al. (2016, *Psychological Science*, 27(7), 1004–1018) replicated in the top 0.01% (259 Duke TIP adolescents): by age 40, 37% had doctorates, 7.5% had academic tenure, and 9% held patents.

**Cognitive epidemiology.** Ian Deary and colleagues pioneered linking childhood intelligence to later health. The Scottish Mental Surveys of 1932 and 1947 tested nearly entire birth-year populations (89,498 children born 1921 tested in 1932; 70,805 born 1936 tested in 1947) on the Moray House Test. Whalley & Deary (2001) found that higher childhood IQ at age 11 predicted longer survival to age 76. The IQ–mortality association is robust; Deary & Der (2005, *Psychological Science*, "Reaction time explains IQ's association with death"; n = 898, age 56 followed to 70) found that "the effect of IQ on mortality was not significant after adjusting for reaction time, suggesting that reduced efficiency of information processing might link lower mental ability and earlier death" — implicating bodily "system integrity." Deary et al. (2000) also documented remarkable stability: IQ at age 11 correlates ~.66–.73 with IQ at age 77–80.

### CHAPTER 3 — Neurophysiology and Brain Basis

**P-FIT.** Jung & Haier (2007, *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 30, 135–154) reviewed 37 neuroimaging studies and proposed the Parieto-Frontal Integration Theory: intelligence depends on a distributed network spanning the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (BAs 6, 9, 10, 45, 46, 47), inferior (BAs 39, 40) and superior (BA 7) parietal lobule, anterior cingulate (BA 32), and temporal/occipital regions, connected by white matter (arcuate fasciculus). It is supported by lesion and multimodal imaging studies and remains the leading regional account.

**Brain volume.** Pietschnig et al. (2015, *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, 57, 411–432) meta-analyzed 88 studies (148 samples, >8,000 individuals) and found brain volume–IQ correlation r = .24 (95% CI [.21, .27], R² ≈ .06), generalizing across age, sex, and IQ domain — but with evidence of publication bias inflating reported effects. Gignac & Bates (2017) argued the true correlation with highly g-loaded tests is ~.31–.40 after correcting for measurement quality; Nave et al. (2019, UK Biobank) found r ≈ .19. The 2022 "meta-analytical multiverse" (Royal Society Open Science) concluded most reasonable estimates cluster in the mid-.20s.

**Neural efficiency.** Haier et al. (1988, *Intelligence*, 12, 199–217) used PET to show that during abstract reasoning (Raven's), regional cerebral glucose metabolic rate correlated negatively with intelligence (correlations between roughly −.48 and −.84 across regions) — smarter brains used less energy. Haier et al. (1992, *Intelligence*, 16, 415–426), the "Tetris" learning study (8 men, ~30–50 days practice), found widespread glucose metabolic decreases after learning, with the largest decreases in higher-ability individuals — the neural efficiency hypothesis. Nuances: efficiency effects are moderated by task difficulty and ability level and do not hold uniformly.

**Network neuroscience.** Finn et al. (2015, *Nature Neuroscience*, 18(11), 1664–1671) used Human Connectome Project data to show functional connectivity "fingerprints" identify individuals (up to 92.9–94.4% accuracy across rest sessions), with the frontoparietal network most distinctive. Using connectome-based predictive modeling (n = 118), predicted vs. observed fluid intelligence correlated r = 0.50 (p < 10⁻⁹) for the positive-feature model and r = 0.26 (p = .005) for the negative-feature model. Barbey (2018, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 22(1), 8–20) proposed the Network Neuroscience Theory: g "emerges from the small-world topology of brain networks and the dynamic reorganization of its community structure," balancing global efficiency (crystallized intelligence, easy-to-reach states) and local efficiency (fluid intelligence/adaptation to novelty).

**The reproducibility correction.** Marek et al. (2022, *Nature*, 603(7902), 654–660) analyzed ~50,000 participants (ABCD, HCP, UK Biobank) and showed that typical brain-wide association studies (with dozens of subjects) are badly underpowered: reliable brain–behavior correlations require thousands of individuals; the median reproducible effect size was ~.01, and published effects of .2+ were inflated. This reframes the entire neuroimaging-of-intelligence literature and counsels caution about small-sample claims.

### CHAPTER 4 — Genetics and Heritability

**Twin/adoption studies and the Wilson effect.** Heritability of intelligence increases with age — the Wilson effect (Bouchard 2013, *Twin Research and Human Genetics*, 16, 923–930). It rises from ~20–25% in early childhood to an asymptote of ~0.80 by age 18–20, sustained into adulthood, while shared-environment influence falls to ~0.10. Briley & Tucker-Drob (2013) and Haworth et al. (2010, ~11,000 twin pairs) corroborate this amplification.

**Molecular genetics.** Plomin & von Stumm (2018, *Nature Reviews Genetics*, 19, 148–159) reviewed "the new genetics of intelligence": ~50% twin heritability, highly polygenic, with individual variants explaining at most ~0.05% of variance. Savage et al. (2018, *Nature Genetics*, 50, 912–919) conducted a GWAS meta-analysis of intelligence in 269,867 individuals, identifying 205 genomic loci and 1,016 genes (implicating neurogenesis and synaptic structure); the resulting polygenic score explained 4.81% of variance in phenotypic intelligence across four independent cohorts. Lee et al. (2018, *Nature Genetics*, 50, 1112–1121) ran a GWAS of educational attainment in ~1.1 million people, identifying 1,271 SNPs; polygenic scores explained 11–13% of variance in educational attainment and 7–10% in cognitive performance. Okbay et al. (2022, *Nature Genetics*, 54(4), 437–449), the EA4 GWAS of ~3 million individuals, identified 3,952 approximately uncorrelated genome-wide-significant SNPs, with a polygenic index explaining 12–16% of EA variance (direct effects explaining roughly half the index's magnitude).

**Genetic nurture and direct vs. indirect effects.** Kong et al. (2018, *Science*, 359, 424–428) showed "genetic nurture": parental alleles NOT transmitted to a child still predict the child's educational attainment, with ~29.9% of the effect of transmitted alleles (P = 1.6×10⁻¹⁴). This means polygenic scores partly capture environmental pathways, not just direct genetic effects — corroborated by Okbay et al.'s (2022) finding that direct effects are roughly half the polygenic index's magnitude.

**Missing heritability and ethics.** The gap between twin heritability (~50%) and SNP/polygenic-score prediction is the "missing heritability." Polygenic prediction is probabilistic, not deterministic; ten percent variance explained equals a correlation of ~.32. Gene-environment correlation (passive/evocative/active; niche-picking) further complicates causal interpretation.

**Gene × SES interaction (contested).** The Scarr-Rowe/Turkheimer hypothesis holds that heritability of intelligence is lower in low-SES environments. Tucker-Drob & Bates (2016) meta-analysis found the interaction in US samples but not consistently in European samples — a partially-replicated, geography-dependent effect, not a settled universal.

### CHAPTER 5 — Environment and Malleability

**The Flynn effect.** Population IQ scores rose throughout the 20th century (James Flynn). The most precise meta-analytic estimate is from Trahan, Stuebing, Fletcher & Hiscock (2014, *Psychological Bulletin*, 140(5), 1332–1360): across 285 studies (N = 14,031) since 1951, the meta-analytic mean gain was 2.31 standard-score points per decade (95% CI [1.99, 2.64]), with ≈2.93 points/decade for modern Stanford-Binet/Wechsler tests. Causes debated: nutrition, education, environmental complexity, smaller families, disease reduction. Crucially, Bratsberg & Rogeberg (2018, *PNAS*, 115(26), 6674–6678) analyzed Norwegian male military conscripts born 1962–1991 and showed the rise, peak (1975 cohort), and subsequent decline (~0.2 points/year) all appear WITHIN families — ruling out genetic/immigration explanations and establishing that both the Flynn effect and its reversal are environmentally caused. (The paper was edited by Richard E. Nisbett.)

**Education raises intelligence.** Ritchie & Tucker-Drob (2018, *Psychological Science*, 29(8), 1358–1369) meta-analyzed 142 effect sizes from 42 datasets (>600,000 participants) across three quasi-experimental designs (control-prior-IQ, compulsory-schooling instrumental variables, regression-discontinuity on school-entry age). An additional year of education caused ~1–5 IQ points of gain, persisting across the lifespan and across ability domains. They concluded education "is the most consistent, robust, and durable method yet to be identified for raising intelligence."

**Brain training (a clear negative finding).** Melby-Lervåg, Redick & Hulme (2016, *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 11(4), 512–534) meta-analyzed 87 publications/145 comparisons: working-memory training reliably improved the trained tasks and near/intermediate transfer (verbal/visuospatial WM) but showed "no convincing evidence" of far transfer to nonverbal ability, verbal ability, reading, or arithmetic when compared with active controls. This rebutted Au et al. (2014). The Simons et al. (2016) consensus and the Owen et al. (2010, *Nature*) large online study reached similar conclusions. **Commercial brain-training claims of general cognitive enhancement are not supported.**

**Deliberate practice (limited explanatory power).** Macnamara, Hambrick & Oswald (2014, *Psychological Science*, 25(8), 1608–1618) meta-analyzed deliberate practice: it explained 26% of variance in games, 21% in music, 18% in sports, 4% in education, and <1% in professions — important but far less than Ericsson's "10,000 hours" framing implies. Most performance variance is explained by other factors (starting age, working memory, genes).

**Other environmental factors.** Early-childhood interventions (Abecedarian, Perry Preschool) show IQ fade-out but persistent gains in life outcomes (Heckman). Lead exposure, iodine deficiency, and iron deficiency demonstrably harm cognition; breastfeeding-IQ effects largely attenuate after controlling for maternal IQ/SES. Physical exercise and cognitive reserve modestly buffer aging-related decline.

### CHAPTER 6 — Contested Areas (presented even-handedly)

**Construct validity.** IQ tests are reliable and predictively valid but measure a sample of cognitive abilities, not the totality of "intelligence"; they poorly capture creativity, practical/tacit knowledge, and rationality.

**Cultural bias and measurement invariance.** Modern psychometric research generally finds measurement invariance across many groups for major test batteries, meaning tests measure the same construct — but predictive and construct fairness remains a serious, active research topic.

**Stereotype threat (replication crisis).** Once a leading explanation for group test-score gaps, stereotype threat is now in question. Flore & Wicherts (2015) meta-analysis found a small effect (d ≈ .22) inflated by publication bias, with corrected effects potentially near zero. Shewach et al. (2019; 212 studies, ~10,000 participants) found small-to-negligible effects under realistic conditions and substantial publication bias. Multiple large replications (Flore et al. 2018) failed. The phenomenon may be real but smaller and more context-dependent than originally claimed.

**Heritability ≠ immutability; is/ought.** High heritability within a population does not mean a trait is unchangeable (height is highly heritable yet rose with nutrition; the Flynn effect proves IQ is environmentally movable). Behavioral-genetic findings describe variance components in specific populations and do not dictate policy (the is/ought distinction).

**History of misuse.** Intelligence testing has a documented history of misuse (eugenics, immigration restriction, forced sterilization) — essential cautionary context, not an argument about the validity of modern measurement.

**Group differences.** Mainstream scientific consensus reviews — the APA's *Intelligence: Knowns and Unknowns* (Neisser et al. 1996, *American Psychologist*, 51, 77–101) and its update (Nisbett et al. 2012, *American Psychologist*, 67(2), 130–159) — emphasize that the causes of average group differences are not established, that environmental factors are strongly implicated, and that no genetic basis for between-group differences has been demonstrated. Nisbett et al. (2012) noted heritability varies by social class and that almost no genetic polymorphisms were then consistently associated with normal-range IQ variation. The honest scientific position is one of genuine uncertainty with the weight of evidence favoring environmental explanations; this report does not advocate any position on causation.

### CHAPTER 7 — Assessment and Measurement

**Instruments.** The major individually-administered tests are the Wechsler scales (WAIS-IV/V, WISC-V), Stanford-Binet 5, Woodcock-Johnson IV, and Kaufman batteries (KABC-II), all structured around CHC theory. Raven's Progressive Matrices is a leading nonverbal Gf measure.

**Psychometrics.** Major IQ tests have high reliability (internal consistency and test-retest typically >.90 for full-scale scores). IQ is normed to a normal distribution (mean 100, SD 15) using large standardization samples, with periodic renorming to counter the Flynn effect. Lifespan stability is high (rank-order stability ~.66–.73 from age 11 to old age in the Scottish cohorts).

**Limits and complements.** Tests measure g and broad abilities well but have practice/coaching effects (modest) and measure motivation, creativity, and practical skills poorly. Elementary cognitive tasks, reaction-time/inspection-time chronometry (Jensen), and working-memory tasks provide convergent process-level measures of the same underlying construct.

## Recommendations
For an author building book chapters from this material, the staged approach:

1. **Anchor each chapter on the strongest evidence first.** Lead with replicated, meta-analytic, or consensus findings: the existence/stability of g (Carroll 1993; Deary cohorts), the Wilson effect (Bouchard 2013), education's causal IQ effect (Ritchie & Tucker-Drob 2018), brain-training's far-transfer failure (Melby-Lervåg et al. 2016), and the GWAS/heritability convergence (Plomin & von Stumm 2018; Savage et al. 2018; Lee et al. 2018; Okbay et al. 2022). These will not be overturned.

2. **Treat the job-performance validity debate as a live controversy, not settled fact.** Present Schmidt & Hunter (1998, r ≈ .51) and the Sackett et al. (2022, ~.31) / Griebe et al. (2022, ~.22) revisions side by side. The benchmark that would change the conclusion: a consensus resolution of the range-restriction-correction dispute (watch for joint Sackett/Ones-Viswesvaran reconciliations and new predictive-design studies).

3. **Apply the Marek et al. (2022) lens to ALL neuroimaging claims.** Flag any brain–intelligence correlation from samples below several hundred as provisional. Cite large-consortium findings (UK Biobank, ABCD, HCP, ENIGMA) preferentially. The threshold for upgrading a neural claim: replication in samples of thousands.

4. **Explicitly separate description from prescription in the contested chapter.** State the is/ought distinction up front; present APA consensus documents (1996, 2012) as the reference points; cite the strongest sources on multiple sides for stereotype threat and gene×SES; avoid any causal claim about group differences beyond "not established, environmental factors strongly implicated, genuine uncertainty remains."

5. **Verify a few citation details against primary PDFs before publication**: Kell et al. (2013) page range (648–659 per DOI); the exact Haier (1988) correlation range against the original *Intelligence* paper; and the Okbay et al. (2022) EA4 sample/variance figures.

## Caveats
- **Source-quality flags**: Several search results came from commercial test-prep/blog sites (cogn-iq.org, plum.io, eskill.com) and AI-generated wikis (Grokipedia); all primary numbers in this report have been cross-checked against peer-reviewed sources (Nature, Science, PNAS, PubMed, journal pages, author PDFs) wherever possible. Do not cite the commercial blogs in the book.
- **The job-performance validity figures are in flux**; any single number (.51, .31, .22) is contestable and method-dependent.
- **Brain–behavior effect sizes are small and historically inflated** by publication bias and underpowered samples; the mid-.20s brain-volume correlation and the Finn r = .50 fluid-intelligence prediction should be read in light of Marek et al. (2022).
- **The Scarr-Rowe gene×SES interaction does not replicate consistently** outside the US; do not present it as universal.
- **One unverified claim was excluded**: a "top 0.01% produced 7× more patents than top 1%" ratio appeared only on an unreliable AI wiki and could not be confirmed in a primary SMPY source; it is omitted.
- This report covers Western, largely European-ancestry samples for most genetic findings (GWAS portability across ancestries is limited) and industrialized democracies for heritability estimates; generalization beyond these populations is not established.
